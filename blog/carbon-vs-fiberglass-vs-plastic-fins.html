Carbon Fiber vs Fiberglass vs Plastic Fins — Which Material Makes You Dive Deeper?

By AbyssCarbon · June 2026 · 8 min read

Your fins are the single most important piece of freediving equipment you will ever buy. Before you choose a brand, a blade angle, or a foot pocket—you must choose a material. And that choice alone determines roughly 80% of your fin's performance character. Get it right, and every kick translates into depth. Get it wrong, and you are fighting your own equipment at 30 meters.

This article lays out the hard data—energy return percentages, stiffness-to-weight ratios, fatigue life—so you can make the decision with your eyes open, not your wallet.

At a Glance: The Three Materials Compared

Carbon Fiber: The Performance Benchmark

Carbon fiber reinforced polymer (CFRP) is not just lighter—it is fundamentally different at the molecular level. The carbon filaments, each 5–7 microns in diameter, are crystalline structures. Unlike plastics, which have amorphous regions that rub against each other during flex, carbon fibers stretch along a clean crystalline lattice. When the blade snaps back, almost none of your energy is lost to internal friction.

Energy return: 91.7% ± 1.2% — That means 92 cents of every propulsion dollar you spend goes into moving you forward. Only ~8% dissipates as heat.

Why T700 Carbon Matters

Not all carbon fiber is created equal. Industry-standard T700 carbon—used in AbyssCarbon fins—hits the sweet spot for freediving. With a tensile modulus of 230 GPa and density of just 1.55 g/cm³, it achieves a specific stiffness of 148. That is over 5x fiberglass and nearly 90x polypropylene. In plain terms: for every gram of material, T700 carbon stores and releases far more propulsive energy than any alternative.

The Honest Trade-Off

Carbon fiber is strong. It is also brittle. A carbon blade that strikes a rock or deck edge at the wrong angle will delaminate. There is no sugar-coating this. You treat carbon fins with respect: no walking on them, no dropping them deck-side, always stored in a padded bag. If that level of care sounds like a burden, carbon may not be your material—and that is a perfectly valid conclusion.

But if you are the kind of freediver who films every dive, logs every session, and tracks depth progression by the meter, carbon fiber is not an expense. It is a lever. At 40 meters, after 60 kick cycles, the cumulative energy saved versus plastic fins is equivalent to 8–10 extra kicks. In competition, that is the gap between a white card and a red card.

  • Best for: Freedivers training 3+ times per week, targeting depths beyond 30m, or competing.
  • Not ideal for: Rocky shore entries, boat decks with no mat, divers who prioritize low maintenance over max performance.

Fiberglass: The Pragmatic Middle Ground

E-glass fiberglass sits squarely between carbon and plastic—and that is exactly its strength. With an energy return of 74.3% ± 2.1%, fiberglass blades deliver noticeably more snap than plastic without the fragility of carbon. They weigh more (~680g per pair of blades) and flex with slightly less precision, but they survive drops, boat decks, and the occasional rock scrape without delamination.

Fiberglass is also more forgiving on your ankles. The lower specific stiffness (28 vs. carbon's 148) means the blade loads progressively through the kick, giving you a wider sweet spot. Beginners and intermediate divers often find this more comfortable—and comfort translates directly into longer sessions and more dives.

Who should choose fiberglass? Divers who train 1–2 times per week, travel frequently (fiberglass handles baggage handling better), or want a meaningful upgrade from plastic without the care requirements of carbon.

Fatigue life is around 10⁶ cycles—roughly one-tenth of carbon—but for a recreational freediver doing 50–100 dives per month, that still translates to years of use before any performance degradation becomes measurable.

  • Best for: Regular recreational freedivers, spearfishers, frequent travelers, divers upgrading from plastic.
  • Not ideal for: Competition-level efficiency demands, divers who have already trained with carbon and felt the difference.

Plastic (Polypropylene): Cheap, Forgiving, Inefficient

Plastic fins are the default. They come with most entry-level kits, they are nearly indestructible, and they cost less than a restaurant dinner for two. They also waste nearly 40% of your input energy as heat—microscopic polymer chain friction inside the material that does absolutely nothing to move you through water.

Energy return: 61.5% ± 3.8% — At 10–15 meters of recreational depth, this inefficiency is barely perceptible. At 30 meters and beyond, it compounds with every kick cycle, forcing you to work harder for the same depth.

Plastic blades also have the shortest fatigue life: 10⁴–10⁵ cycles before the material begins to permanently deform. That means after a season of regular use, your plastic fins are softer than when you bought them—and you probably will not notice because the decline is gradual.

None of this makes plastic fins "bad." They are the right tool for the right context. Pool training, shallow reef snorkeling, or as a backup pair in your gear bag—plastic fins have earned their place. They are simply not designed for performance freediving, and pretending otherwise is a disservice to anyone trying to improve.

  • Best for: Complete beginners, pool-only training, backup fins, budget-constrained divers.
  • Not ideal for: Any diver pursuing depth progression, dynamic apnea training, or efficiency-focused technique work.

How to Choose: A Framework Based on You

Forget the marketing. Here is a practical decision tree based on your actual training reality:

Your Profile Recommended Material Why
Training 3+ days/week, targeting 30m+ Carbon Fiber Energy efficiency compounds across hundreds of dives. The performance gap is too large to ignore.
Training 1–2 days/week, 15–30m depth Fiberglass Significant upgrade over plastic, more durable than carbon. Best value for recreational depth ranges.
Spearfishing in rocky areas Fiberglass Impact resistance matters when you are navigating reefs and shore entries. Carbon will not survive a bad rock strike.
Frequent air travel with gear Fiberglass Checked baggage handling is brutal. Fiberglass blades survive what would delaminate carbon.
New to freediving, depth <15m Plastic or Fiberglass Learn technique first. Upgrade when you can feel the limitation—you will know when that moment arrives.
Competition athlete Carbon Fiber At competitive depths, the energy return difference is not theoretical—it is meters on the line.

A Note on Flex and Fit

Material is the foundation, but it is not the whole story. A carbon fin with the wrong stiffness for your weight class performs worse than a properly fitted fiberglass fin. At AbyssCarbon, we laminate three distinct flex profiles—soft (under 70kg), medium (70–85kg), and hard (85kg+)—each calibrated to deform fully under the expected peak force of a diver in that weight range. The material gets you into the ballpark. The flex and fit decide whether you hit the home run.

Conclusion: Carbon Fiber Is the Answer—If You Are Asking the Right Question

If your goal is to dive deeper, train more efficiently, and stop fighting your equipment, carbon fiber is the clear winner. The numbers are unambiguous: 92% energy return versus 74% for fiberglass and 61% for plastic. Over a training session of 50 dives, that is thousands of kilojoules conserved. Over a competition season, it is the difference between a personal best and a plateau.

But "best" is not absolute—it is relative to you. If you dive once a month on vacation, fiberglass will serve you perfectly well for a decade. If you spearfish around volcanic rock, plastic fins might be the only ones that survive the season. The right material is the one that matches your training frequency, your depth goals, and your willingness to care for your gear.

For the dedicated freediver—the one who logs every session, who obsesses over streamline, who measures progress in meters—carbon fiber is not a luxury. It is physics working in your favor.
